The borrow checker isn't your enemy — it's a design critic

Every Rust beginner has the same experience: write some seemingly reasonable code, get a wall of borrow checker errors, feel like the language is working against you. I had this experience. My colleagues had this experience. It's nearly universal.

What took me a while to internalize is that the borrow checker isn't arbitrary — it's encoding a formal model of ownership and aliasing. When it rejects your code, it's telling you something about the structure of your program.

Fighting the borrow checker is a design smell

In my experience, the borrow checker fights hardest when I'm trying to express something that has a genuinely awkward ownership structure. A struct that holds a reference to data it doesn't own. A callback that captures a mutable reference into a closure that outlives the data. Shared state threaded through a graph of objects.

These are all patterns that are technically possible in other languages — but they're also the patterns that produce use-after-free bugs, data races, and heisenbugs in those languages. Rust just makes the cost visible at compile time.

Reading the error as feedback

When I stopped treating borrow checker errors as obstacles and started treating them as design feedback, my Rust code got better. A fight with the borrow checker over mutable aliasing often resolves cleanly when you rethink ownership: does this object really need to hold that reference? Can I pass the data through instead?

The classic transformation is moving from "object A holds a reference to object B" to "function receives both A and B". It feels like a downgrade — less elegant, more verbose — but it usually exposes that the original design had implicit coupling that would have caused problems anyway.

The actual enemy

The enemy isn't the borrow checker. It's the habit of porting designs from GC'd languages directly into Rust without reconsidering ownership. Rust idioms feel foreign at first because they're solving a problem the language takes seriously that other languages paper over with a garbage collector. Once that clicks, the borrow checker becomes a collaborator.
