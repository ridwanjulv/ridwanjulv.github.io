What I look for when hiring senior engineers

After running interviews for dozens of senior engineering roles, I've mostly stopped caring about algorithm puzzles. What I look for instead is a set of signals that predict how someone will actually perform in the work — which looks nothing like a LeetCode session.

Comfort with ambiguity

Senior engineers are given problems, not tickets. The most important signal I look for is how a candidate responds when I give them an underspecified design problem. Do they ask clarifying questions? Do they identify and name the ambiguity before proposing a solution? Or do they immediately start designing, filling in assumptions without surfacing them?

The best candidates treat ambiguity as information. "This question has multiple valid answers depending on the consistency requirements" is a better first sentence than a system diagram.

Articulate trade-off reasoning

I don't care much which option a candidate chooses. I care a lot about whether they can articulate why. "I'd use Postgres here because the team knows it and the data model is relational" is a good answer. "I'd use Postgres here because it's the best database" is a flag.

How they talk about their failures

Ask about a production incident they were involved in. Senior engineers take ownership clearly, describe what they learned specifically, and can trace the systemic factors that made the failure possible. They don't deflect blame, but they also don't perform excessive guilt. It's an incident, not a character judgment.

What I've stopped caring about

Memorised API signatures. Whiteboard sorts. Whether they know the exact syntax of a language they haven't used in two years. These things measure preparation for the interview, not performance in the role.
