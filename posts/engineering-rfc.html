How to write an engineering RFC that people actually read

Most RFCs are too long. The second most common failure mode is being too vague. Both problems have the same root cause: the author isn't sure who they're writing for, so they write for everyone — and end up writing for no one.

Start with the problem, not the solution

The single most effective structural change you can make is spending the first third of your RFC on the problem statement. Not the background. Not the history. The specific, observable problem you are trying to solve. If you can't write this in three paragraphs without hedging, you don't understand the problem well enough to propose a solution.

The decision section is the document

Most readers will skip to the part where you tell them what you're proposing. Make that section explicit and early. A one-paragraph summary of the proposed change — what it is, not why — should appear before your alternatives section. Give readers the anchor before they read the justification.

Alternatives aren't a formality

The alternatives section is where you demonstrate that you've actually thought about the problem. A weak alternatives section (one option, described dismissively) signals that the author has already decided and is going through the motions. A strong alternatives section describes each option fairly and explains the trade-off that led you away from it.

What to cut

  • History sections that read like a project post-mortem
  • Diagrams added to fill space rather than clarify
  • References to Jira tickets that 80% of reviewers can't access
  • Any sentence starting with "As we all know"

The best RFC I ever read was four pages. It defined the problem, proposed a change, listed three alternatives with honest trade-offs, and described how success would be measured. It got approved in 48 hours. Length is not rigor.
