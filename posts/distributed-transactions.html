What I got wrong about distributed transactions (and what finally clicked)

For a long time I thought distributed transactions were mostly solved. Two-phase commit exists. Saga patterns exist. XA transactions exist. How hard could it be?

Turns out: very. And the failure wasn't in the protocols themselves — it was in my mental model of what "correctness" means across network boundaries.

The mistake: treating the network like a function call

When you call a local function, you get a result. When you call a remote service, you get one of three outcomes: success, failure, or unknown. That third state is the one that breaks naive transaction models.

A coordinator in 2PC can crash after sending PREPARE but before sending COMMIT. Participants are now blocked — they've locked resources, they're waiting, and they can't unilaterally decide to commit or abort without risking inconsistency. This is the classic 2PC blocking problem.

What finally clicked: failure as a first-class value

The shift that helped me was treating partial failure as a value in the type system of the system design, not an edge case. Every operation that crosses a process boundary either:

  • Succeeded
  • Failed cleanly (and the failure was observed)
  • Failed ambiguously (you don't know what happened)

Sagas handle this by making every step compensatable. Rather than relying on atomicity, you design for eventual consistency with explicit rollback logic. It's more code, but the failure modes are predictable.

The practical takeaway

If you're designing a system that needs cross-service consistency, ask: can I make each step idempotent? Can I make each step compensatable? If yes to both, Sagas will serve you better than 2PC in most production environments. If you truly need serializable isolation across services, you're probably looking at a coordination service like ZooKeeper or etcd — and you should know exactly why you need it before reaching for it.
