One-on-ones are the highest-leverage recurring meeting in most engineering orgs, and also the most consistently wasted. They default to status updates, which is both unnecessary (that's what async tools are for) and a missed opportunity.

This post is about what makes one-on-ones actually useful — for both the engineer and the manager.

## The default trap

The path of least resistance in a one-on-one is: engineer gives status update, manager nods, both leave feeling like they did the meeting right. Nothing surfaces, nothing changes, and within six months the meeting feels like an obligation rather than an investment.

Status updates belong in Slack, Jira, or a doc. The one-on-one is for things that don't fit anywhere else.

## What one-on-ones are actually for

- **Surfacing blockers early** — things that are slowing you down before they become critical
- **Career direction** — where you want to go, what skills you're building, what stretch you want
- **Feedback in both directions** — what's going well, what isn't
- **Building enough trust to have honest conversations** — this one takes time and is the foundation for all the others

## Structure that helps

A simple format that keeps things focused:

1. **What's on your mind?** (5 min) — open-ended, let the other person set the agenda
2. **Wins since last time** (2 min) — deliberate acknowledgment, easy to skip and worth not skipping
3. **What's in the way?** (5 min) — blockers, friction, things that shouldn't be their job but are
4. **Career topic** (5 min) — one topic per meeting; rotate through goals, feedback, growth areas
5. **Action items** (2 min) — what does each person commit to before next time

The meeting doesn't need to follow this exactly. The structure exists to prevent the whole 30 minutes from being eaten by project status.

## As the engineer

Come prepared. Bring the two or three things that are actually on your mind. If you're waiting to be asked, you're leaving value on the table.

Write down what you want from the next quarter of your career. Bring it up once a month. If your manager doesn't know you want to lead a project, they can't help you get one.

When something is frustrating — a process, a decision, a relationship — surface it in a one-on-one, not after you've already decided to leave.

## As the manager

The most important thing you can do is create enough safety that people tell you the real answer when you ask "what's in the way?" This takes time and consistent behavior. People test whether it's safe to be honest.

Take notes. Review them before each meeting. Nothing signals "this is a real conversation" like remembering what someone said three weeks ago.

## The long game

A well-run one-on-one series is essentially a continuous record of someone's development. You can trace how their priorities shifted, what stuck, what they said they wanted and whether they got it.

That record is also useful when it's time to write a performance review, make a case for a promotion, or give a reference.
